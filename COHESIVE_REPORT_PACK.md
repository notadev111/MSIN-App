# Acumen Cohesive Report Pack

This file is the working bridge between the research, MVP, diagrams, survey questions, and eventual report. It is not the report. It is the agreed logic so the report can be written quickly and coherently.

## 1. Recommended Positioning

Use this positioning:

**Acumen is a simulation-based platform that helps young people practise business judgment, receive structured leadership feedback, and connect with peers who complement their strengths.**

Sharper version for the report:

**Acumen helps emerging business leaders build decision-making confidence through realistic simulations based on APAC family-business succession, governance, and strategic pressure.**

Why this is stronger:
- It is broad enough for student surveys and early adoption.
- It is specific enough to avoid sounding like another generic leadership app.
- It lets the family-business research drive the scenarios without making the whole venture depend only on wealthy heirs.
- It makes the MVP easier to defend: students can test the product now, while NextGen family business leaders become a later premium segment.

Avoid this positioning:

**An app only for people inheriting family businesses.**

Why: this makes the market feel small, private, hard to access, and hard to validate before submission.

Best framing:

**Beachhead users:** business students, young professionals, entrepreneurship society members, and future founders.

**Strategic premium users:** NextGen family business successors and family-enterprise leadership programmes.

**Research anchor:** APAC family-business succession, governance, and leadership readiness.

This should be stated clearly in the report. The app is not only for heirs inheriting companies. The family-business research gives the product a serious, high-stakes context, while the first users are easier to reach: students, young professionals, and aspiring founders who want to build business acumen.

## 1A. Exemplar Report Lessons

The exemplar report is useful because it does not try to explain everything in prose. It uses a clear title page, table of contents, short main sections, diagrams, figures, prototype screenshots, customer evidence, appendices, and a bibliography.

What to copy from the exemplar:
- Open with a clear venture definition.
- Use a scope section to explain exactly what the report will and will not assess.
- Put the canvas early so readers understand the business model quickly.
- Use visuals for frameworks instead of long blocks of text.
- Put raw interview evidence, meeting notes, screenshots, and extra detail in appendices.
- End with an explicit feasibility judgement.

What to improve on compared with the exemplar:
- Make the writing more concise and less repetitive.
- Keep the word count under 3,000.
- Make every framework connect back to the same core venture logic.
- Avoid long descriptive paragraphs for SWOT and Porter.

Suggested section lengths for a 3,000 word report:

| Section | Target words | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Executive summary | 250 to 300 | Problem, solution, target users, evidence, feasibility verdict |
| Scope and feasibility questions | 250 to 300 | What the report tests and why |
| Product definition and MVP | 350 to 450 | What Acumen does, who it is for, screenshots, MVP scope |
| Market and research context | 500 to 650 | APAC succession, leadership readiness, serious games, assessment logic |
| Customer validation | 400 to 500 | Survey and interview findings once collected |
| Business model and value proposition | 350 to 450 | BMC and VPC summary, not full explanation |
| Market attractiveness, SWOT, Porter | 450 to 600 | Visual frameworks plus key implications |
| Feasibility verdict and next steps | 350 to 450 | Limitations, recommendations, SMART next steps |

Appendices should hold:
- Full SWOT and Porter detail.
- Full survey/interview questions.
- Raw or summarised responses.

## 2. Simple Product Story

The product story should always move in this order:

1. Young leaders need practice making complex business decisions.
2. Traditional learning often teaches concepts, but does not show how someone thinks under pressure.
3. Family-business succession in APAC is a high-stakes context where leadership judgment really matters.
4. Acumen turns those pressures into short simulations.
5. The simulation produces a leadership signal.
6. That signal helps users reflect, improve, and find complementary peers.

The loop:

**Simulate -> Measure -> Match**

Do not over-explain the app. Keep returning to that loop.

## 3. What The MVP Is

The MVP is enough.

Current MVP:
- Onboarding.
- Three simulation scenarios.
- Stakeholder information.
- Ethical decision prompts.
- Hidden scoring rubric.
- Leadership feedback.
- Bias profile.
- Dashboard.
- Profile.
- Peer network page.

What the MVP proves:
- Users can complete a decision simulation in one session.
- Choices can be translated into a useful leadership profile.
- Feedback can lead to a next action, such as peer discovery.
- The core loop can be demonstrated without CV parsing, map visualisation, or live matching.

What is deliberately out of scope:
- CV upload and parsing.
- World map network visualisation.
- Star ratings for connection strength.
- Real-time messaging.
- Algorithmic matching at scale.
- Empirically validated psychometric scoring.

How to phrase this in the report:

**These features were excluded from the MVP because they test later-stage scalability and data integration, not the core desirability question. The first feasibility question is whether users find simulation-based leadership feedback useful and credible.**

## 4. Research Questions That Match The Concept

Use these as the spine of the report:

1. Do emerging business leaders feel underprepared for complex leadership decisions?
2. Do realistic simulations feel like a useful way to practise business judgment?
3. Do users trust structured feedback from simulation choices?
4. Does skill-based peer matching feel more meaningful than generic networking?
5. Is the family-business succession context a strong enough scenario theme to make the product distinctive?

These questions match both audiences:
- Students can answer them.
- Young professionals can answer them.
- Family-business respondents can add richer context.

The market validation does match the concept if the concept is framed correctly:

**Primary validation target:** business students, young professionals, and aspiring founders.

**What they validate:** whether the core loop is useful, credible, and desirable.

**Secondary validation target:** people with family-business exposure.

**What they validate:** whether the succession and governance scenario context feels realistic and valuable.

Do not ask every respondent about inheriting a family business. That makes the survey too narrow. Instead, ask all respondents about business judgment, leadership confidence, feedback, and peer matching. Then use one screener question to identify whether any respondent has family-business exposure.

## 7. Human SWOT

Use this as a concise 2x2 table or diagram in the report. Do not use long text in the main body. Put the fuller explanation in an appendix if needed.

Recommended main-report format:

| Strengths | Weaknesses |
| --- | --- |
| Focused simulation, feedback, and matching loop | Limited primary validation so far |
| Research-backed scoring logic | Scoring not yet empirically calibrated |
| Distinctive APAC succession context | Network value depends on user density |

| Opportunities | Threats |
| --- | --- |
| Students and young professionals want practical leadership experience | Generic learning platforms have trust and distribution |
| Universities can pilot the product cheaply | Users may distrust automated or unclear feedback |
| NextGen family business successors create a premium later segment | Succession and family governance are sensitive topics |

Use 120 to 180 words after the table to explain the key implication:

**The SWOT suggests that Acumen's near-term strength is product clarity, not validated defensibility. The immediate priority is to prove that users trust simulation feedback and find the peer-matching layer useful. If this is validated with students first, the APAC family-business context can become a premium scenario pathway rather than a narrow launch constraint.**

### Strengths

**A focused learning loop**
Acumen does not just teach leadership theory. It asks users to make decisions, then gives them feedback they can act on.

**Research-backed scenarios**
The simulations are grounded in recognised frameworks, including Upper Echelons Theory, Vroom-Yetton, OODA, Rest's ethical model, and behavioural economics.

**A distinctive APAC context**
The family-business succession angle gives the product a sharper identity than a generic leadership app.

### Weaknesses

**Limited primary validation**
The strongest evidence is still secondary research. More surveys and interviews are needed to prove real demand.

**Scoring is not yet calibrated**
The feedback is structured and research-informed, but it is not yet a validated assessment instrument.

**Network value depends on scale**
Peer matching becomes much more valuable when there are enough active users.

### Opportunities

**Leadership readiness is a real gap**
Students and young professionals want experience, feedback, and confidence before facing real business pressure.

**APAC succession is a high-stakes use case**
Family-business transition, governance, and NextGen readiness create a strong premium market later.

**Universities are a natural pilot channel**
Entrepreneurship centres, business societies, and leadership modules could test the product with low distribution cost.

### Threats

**Generic learning platforms can copy parts of the product**
LinkedIn Learning, Coursera, business schools, and coaching platforms already own trust and distribution.

**Users may distrust automated feedback**
If the scoring feels vague or overconfident, the product loses credibility.

**The family-business angle is sensitive**
Succession, governance, and family conflict are private topics, so trust and privacy must be designed carefully.

## 8. Human Porter's Five Forces

Use this as a one-page diagram or compact table. Each force should be a short box, not a long essay. The main report should show the force, rating, and implication. The appendix can hold the longer version.

Recommended main-report format:

| Force | Rating | Key implication |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Competitive rivalry | Moderate | No exact direct competitor, but many indirect alternatives exist |
| Threat of entrants | Moderate | Technology is copyable, but credible content and validation are harder |
| Supplier power | Low to moderate | AI and hosting are easy to switch, expert scenario knowledge matters |
| Buyer power | Moderate | Students have many free alternatives, institutions demand proof |
| Substitutes | High | Courses, mentors, internships, business societies, and doing nothing all compete |

Use 120 to 180 words after the table:

**Porter's Five Forces suggests that Acumen should not compete as a generic education app. Its advantage has to come from a focused scenario library, trusted feedback, and partnership-based distribution. The most important substitute is not another app, but the user's existing habit of learning informally through courses, clubs, mentors, or experience. This is why the MVP must create value in one short session.**

### Competitive Rivalry: Moderate

There is no obvious direct competitor combining business simulations, leadership scoring, and peer matching. The real competition is indirect: business schools, LinkedIn, Coursera, coaching, mentoring, and learning through experience.

Implication:
Acumen must prove that simulation feedback is more useful than passive learning.

### Threat of New Entrants: Moderate

The technology is not hard to copy. A basic simulation app can be built quickly. The harder parts are credible scenarios, trusted scoring, cultural context, and user community.

Implication:
The defence is not the interface. It is content quality, validation, and partnerships.

### Supplier Power: Low to Moderate

Cloud tools and AI APIs are widely available. The real supplier risk is expert knowledge from scenario writers, family-business advisors, and assessment specialists.

Implication:
Acumen should build repeatable scenario templates and seek academic or practitioner review.

### Buyer Power: Moderate

Individual users have many alternatives and may not pay without proof of value. Institutions have more budget, but they will demand evidence, credibility, and privacy safeguards.

Implication:
Start with a free or university-backed pilot, then build evidence for paid institutional use.

### Threat of Substitutes: High

Users can choose courses, internships, mentors, MBAs, business societies, or simply learn by doing. The biggest substitute is doing nothing.

Implication:
Acumen needs to feel fast, useful, and credible in one session.

## 13. Report Tone Rules

Make it human:
- Shorter sentences.
- Fewer buzzwords.
- Explain why each framework matters.
- Admit limitations clearly.
- Avoid pretending the MVP is more validated than it is.

Avoid:
- Long textbook paragraphs.
- Overclaiming psychometric validity.
- Saying the product is only for rich heirs.
- Treating missing map/CV features as failures.
- Too many frameworks without linking them to product decisions.

Use:
- "This suggests..."
- "The MVP tests..."
- "The next step is..."
- "The main uncertainty is..."
- "Our current evidence supports..."

## 14. Current Feasibility Verdict

Acumen is coherent enough to start writing the report once primary research is added. The venture is strongest when framed as a business judgment practice platform for young emerging leaders, using APAC family-business succession as the distinctive scenario context.

The app does not need to become more complex for the MVP. The report should explain that the MVP intentionally tests the core learning and feedback loop before adding heavier features like CV parsing, map visualisation, and live matching.
