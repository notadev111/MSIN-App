Indonesian culture highlight
Paternalistic systems can create supplier dependence: suppliers may prefer stability + protection via the “father figure” leadership model. 
Harmful toward business progress
When supplier relationships are relational/political, you risk opaque pricing, related-party transactions, and lower productivity from weaker market discipline.
Succession pointer
Supplier leverage rises during transitions: uncertainty lets banks/suppliers demand tougher terms, especially if succession is delayed or contested. 
4) Buyer Power
Buyers include consumers, corporate customers, and (in many sectors) the government as a buyer (procurement).
Economic points
Buyer power is lower when conglomerates control distribution channels/land/market access; higher when buyers can switch to imports/digital alternatives.
“Banks and MFOs have been at the forefront of serving Asia–Pacific’s wealthy families. However, other types of providers are expanding into the family office space to target the needs of wealthy families in line with consumer demand. Insurers, in particular, are setting up teams to directly engage with family offices to provide highly customized solutions with a focus on estate planning; asset managers often receive direct mandates from family offices for traditional and alternative investments; and WealthTechs are gaining traction in the family offices ecosystem. This results not only in innovation for customers but also in the opportunity for providers such as banks and insurers to collaborate and expand their offerings to meet this segment’s needs.” Mckinsey article 
“Reputation” and “purpose” are increasingly framed as competitive levers in family businesses (buyers care about trust, ESG, quality).
Greater information advantage:
Banks have family fund structures in-house, providing single-family offices with comprehensive services at competitive costs, and single-family offices integrated financial services, including access to research and various credit and investment products.
“For example: DBS Private Bank has launched the DBS Multi Family Office Foundry VCC (DBS MFO), which is, in DBS’s words, “the world’s first bank-backed multi family office that leverages Singapore’s Variable Capital Company (VCC) structure. It offers an alternative option for affluent families to manage their wealth in Singapore, without having to establish their own Single Family Office. Through DBS MFO, clients will be able to access a full suite of investment services—from investment management, trade execution, to custody solutions—via a single integrated platform”
Indonesian culture highlight
Loyalty norms (family + long-standing relationships) show up as a reported strength in Indonesian family firms, this can reduce buyer churn. 
Harmful toward business progress
Buyer power can be artificially weakened by patronage networks (buyers “locked” by relationships), which can reduce pressure to improve quality/price.
Succession pointer
NextGen often has the best shot at rebuilding buyer trust through modern governance, transparency, and digital service, but only if they’re actually empowered (senior resistance is the blocker). 
5) Threat of Substitutes
Substitutes = new business models that bypass old networks: tech platforms, cross-border competitors, decentralised finance, direct-to-consumer, etc.
Economic points
ASEAN conglomerates’ low exposure to tech/healthcare is exactly where substitutes emerge fastest, making disruption a real strategic threat. 
PwC’s family business survey framing: disruption rewards firms with purpose + agility + governance; performance is diverging, and only ~1 in 4 hit double-digit growth globally. 
In 2024, “only 9% of Asia Pacific family businesses having established protocols on the responsible use of emerging technologies like AI” link. 
Indonesian culture highlight
Bapakism’s authoritarian/paternal character is explicitly described as unlikely to support certain modern leadership needs (i.e., adaptability/psychological safety), which can worsen substitute risk. 
Harmful toward business progress
Substitutes win when incumbents are too conservative, overly centralised, or treat disruption as “risk” rather than opportunity.
The NextGen in Asia Pacific have more pronounced trust issues: “More than three-quarters (76%) of them believe there is a low level of trust between NextGen family members and the current generation” link
“80% of these future stewards also feel that there is a lack of trust between family and non-family members”
Rising operational costs: “personnel costs are typically the largest expense category for a family office, accounting for about 45 to 65 percent of operating expenses for a single-family office with $15 million to $500 million in AUM”
“There is increased attrition in talent, especially in less professionalized family offices. Greater efforts are needed to improve retention because when professionals are removed from structured environments like banks, they lose access to critical information, knowledge bases, and networks, which may prompt them to seek new employment opportunities.”said Aik-Ping Ng, head of family office advisory, Asia Pacific, HSBC, and Edith Ang, head of family advisory, Asia Pacific, HSBC Global Private Banking link
Family offices in the Asia–Pacific region… they have limited access to tailored alternative investment solutions such as access to early-stage start-ups, pre-IPOs, and institutional-grade infrastructure solutions, despite strong interest Mckinsey Article  
Asia–Pacific’s family offices currently have limited engagement with insurers
“educate families on the potential of insurance to enable families in their wealth transfer and preservation process, such as insurance policies covering multiple generations of beneficiaries across the globe, or policies providing liquidity during estate transfers”
Succession pointer
Practical “anti-substitute” succession cues: structured succession planning, governance that allows faster leadership transition, and openness to professional/female leadership are repeatedly emphasised. 
Cultivate relationships: “collaborate with other professionals, such as tax advisers and legal advisers” Mckinsey Article
Insurers can build an ecosystem for value-added services: tax advisory, immigration support while serving families in multiple jurisdictions, legal support, etc
 Access to the private market and alternative investments is a massive opportunity… education is essential to enable family offices to invest in this area effectively
Depending on the family business’s AUM, technology costs are about 3-7% of total expenditures, so more calls for efficient data management and operational execution: service providers to streamline onboarding experience, and enabling data-driven insights for greater customer engagement.
